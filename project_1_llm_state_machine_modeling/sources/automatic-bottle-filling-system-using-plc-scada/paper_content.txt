  This project presents an Automatic Bottle Filling System using Siemens S7 -
1200 PLC and SCADA for real -time monitoring. It uses an IR sensor to 
detect bottles, an ultrasonic sensor to check liquid levels, and a mini pump 
to fill the bottles. The conveyor is controlled using DC motors and relays, 
with logic programmed via TIA Portal V16. The system improves 
accuracy, reduces manual effort, and is suitable for small to medium -scale 
industries like beverages and dairy. It provides an efficient, automated 
solution for modern production lines.

INTRODUCTION  
In today’s fast -paced industrial world, automation 
plays a crucial role in improving productivity, 
reducing manual errors, and ensuring consistency in 
manufacturing processes. One such widely used 
automation application is the automatic bottle filling 
system, which is highly relevant in industries such as 
food and beverages, pharmaceuticals, chemicals, and 
dairy. Manual bottle filling processes are often s low, 
inaccu - rate, and prone to spillage, leading to product 
wastage and labor fatigue. To overcome these 
challenges, this project focuses on designing and 
implementing an Automatic Bottle Filling System 
using Programmable Logic Controller (PLC) and 
Superv isory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA). 
The proposed system uses a Siemens S7 -1200 PLC 
programmed using TIA Portal V16, enabling precise control over the sequence of operations. An IR sensor 
is used to detect the presence of a bottle on the 
conveyor, w hile an ultrasonic sen - sor monitors the 
liquid level inside the bottle. Once the bottle is in 
position, the conveyor halts, and a mini water pump 
is activated to fill the bottle. After reaching the 
desired level, the pump stops, and the conveyor 
resumes, moving the filled bottle forward. The entire 
system is monitored through a SCADA interface that 
allows real -time observation, parameter control, and 
status feedback. This integration ensures efficiency, 
reduces manpower, and enhances accuracy in filling 
operations. The system is cost -effective, scalable, and 
designed to suit small to medium -scale production 
units. Overall, it demonstrates how industrial 
automa - tion can enhance production quality while

PROBLEM STATEMENT  
Manual bottle filling in small and medium industries 
often leads to issues like uneven filling, spillage, slow 
processing, and higher labor costs. These challenges 
affect productivity, accuracy, and hygiene. Without 
automation, scaling up o perations or ensuring real -
time monitoring becomes difficult. To overcome these 
limitations, there is a need for an efficient, automated 
system that can handle bottle detection, filling, and 
monitoring with minimal human intervention.

Solution Statement  
To address the limitation s of manual bottle filling, 
this project proposes an automated bottle filling 
system using a Siemens S7 -1200 PLC integrated with 
SCADA for real -time monitoring and control. The 
system uses an IR sensor to detect the presence of 
bottles on the conveyor and an ultrasonic sensor to 
monitor the liquid level during filling. A mini water 
pump is used for dispensing the liquid, while a 
conveyor mechanism con - trolled by DC motors 
moves the bottles through the process. All 
components are connected to the PLC, which  is 
programmed using TIA Portal V16 to execute the 
filling sequence with precision. The SCADA interface enables visualization, live status updates, and manual 
overrides if necessary. This automated setup ensures 
consistent fill levels, reduces human effort , improves 
hygiene, and increases production speed —making it 
an ideal solution for small and medium -scale 
industries.

Methodology  
The methodology of this project involves designing 
and implementing an automated bottle filling system 
by integrating sensors, actuators, and a PLC -based 
control unit. First, the components such as the IR 
sensor, ultrasonic sensor, mini water pump, and 
conveyor mechanism are selected and connected to 
the Siemens S7 -1200 PLC. The system is powered 
through a suitable SMPS, and all connections are 
established following standard wiring practices. Using 
TIA Portal V16, a ladder logic program is developed 
to cont rol the sequence of operations —starting from 
bottle detection, halting the conveyor, initiating the 
filling process, checking the fill level, and restarting

Software  
The automated bottle filling system can be effectively 
developed and simulated using TIA Portal V13, a 
com- prehensive software environment designed for 
programming, simulating, and monitoring PLC -based 
au- tomation systems. TIA Portal V13 supports the 
Siemens S7 -1200 PLC, which is widely used for 
industrial automation projects due to its reliability 
and advanced features. This software enables the 
creation of ladder logic programs, integration of 
sensors and actuators, and simulation of the entire 
process without requiring physical hardware.

The process begins by defining the inputs and outputs 
in TIA Portal. Inputs  include sensors for detecting 
bottle presence and size, while outputs control the 
conveyor motor, solenoid valve for liquid flow, and 
other actuators involved in the process. For example, 
height sensors are used to distinguish between small 
(200 ml) and l arge (500 ml) bottles. These sensors 
generate binary data (0 for small bottles and 1 for 
large bottles), which is stored in a shift register.  
The program logic involves tracking bottles using a 
shift register. Each filling station corresponds to a data 
bit in the register. As bottles move along the 
conveyor, their size information is shifted through 
the register. When a bottle reaches the filling station, 
the program checks its corresponding bit value: a ”1” 
triggers long filling (e.g., 4 seconds), while ”0 ” triggers 
short filling (e.g., 2 seconds). The shift operation 
continues until all stations are filled. After completing 
the filling cycle for eight bottles (based on an 8 -bit 
register), the system resets the register and starts a 
new cycle.  
To implement this in TIA Portal V13:  
Define Inputs/Outputs: Configure sensors as inputs 
(e.g., I0.0 for bottle detection) and actuators as 
outputs (e.g., Q0.0 for conveyor control).  
Create Program Logic: Use ladder diagrams to 
implement shift operations, timers, counte rs, and 
conditional statements for controlling filling 
durations based on bottle size.  
Simulation: Utilize the integrated PLC simulator to 
test program functionality without physical 
hardware.  
Optimization: Adjust timing parameters (e.g., 
conveyor speed or  filling duration) based on 
simulation re - sults to ensure smooth operation.  
Deployment: Once verified through simulation, 
deploy the program to an actual S7 -1200 PLC for real -
world application.  
 The use of TIA Portal V13 simplifies complex

Advantages and Disadvantages  
The automated bottle filling and capping sy stem 
offers several advantages, making it an efficient 
solution for industrial production. One of the primary 
benefits is its ability to significantly improve 
productivity by re - ducing manual intervention. 
Automation ensures consistent filling and capping , 
minimizing human errors and maintaining uniformity 
across batches. Additionally, the integration of 
sensors and PLC -based control systems enhances 
precision, ensuring accurate liquid dispensing and 
proper capping. This system also re - duces labor costs 
and increases operational speed, making it ideal for 
large -scale production environments. Furthermore, 
automation contributes to better hygiene standards by 
limiting direct contact with bottles, which is 
particularly advantageous in industries like food and  
beverage or pharmaceuticals.  
However, the system has certain disadvantages that 
must be considered. The initial setup cost of such 
auto- mated systems can be high due to the need for 
advanced hardware components like sensors, motors, 
solenoid valves, and PLCs. Maintenance can also be 
challenging, as any malfunction in the hardware or 
software may require specialized expertise to resolve. 
Additionally, the system’s reliance on electricity

Future Scope  
The automated bottle filling and capping system holds 
significant potential for future advancements, par - 
ticularly with the integration of Industry 4.0

The automated bottle filling and capping system
