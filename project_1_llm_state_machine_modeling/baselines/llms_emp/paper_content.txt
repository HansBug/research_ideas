Abstract
Model-driven development (MDD) is a mainstream approach in
safety-critical domains, providing standardized modeling languages
like SysML. SysML behavior models describe system dynamics and
are widely used in aerospace, manufacturing, and IoT. However,
manual modeling is inefficient and prone to quality issues, restrict-
ing MDD’s practical adoption. The potential of LLMs in SysML
behavior model generation and its challenges remain unclear, mak-
ing it a key research topic. This empirical study evaluates LLMs
in generating three types of SysML behavior models, focusing on
performance and hallucinations. Our contributions are twofold:
(1) constructing and publishing a dataset of 107 SysML behavior
models spanning various domains; (2) analyzing hallucinations in
LLM-assisted SysML behavior model generation from syntactic and
semantic perspectives and proposing model-checking rules to miti-
gate them and enhance model quality. We analyze hallucinations in
SysML behavior model generation, classifying them and exploring
their possible causes. The evaluation results show that while the
models generally meet syntactic requirements, they consistently
lack semantic accuracy. Across both phases, LLMs achieve over 90%
grammar accuracy. For semantic accuracy, the average F1-score
for ACT reaches 95%, while SD drops to just 50%. These results
demonstrate that while our model-checking rules effectively cor-
rect format and syntax, they are insufficient for addressing deeper
semantic gaps. Overcoming these challenges requires advanced
strategies, such as counterexamples and simulation traces, to pro-
vide optimal feedback. Additionally, model-checking in LLM-based
generation is costly, and reducing this cost is another critical issue
to address in the future.

1 Introduction
Model-driven development (MDD) [ 63] is a key approach for de-
signing safety-critical complex systems [ 21,26,34,40]. SysML, a
standardized systems modeling language, supports requirements,
design, and verification and validation (V&V) [ 30,59,61,76]. Its
behavioral diagrams—use case (UCD), state machine (STM), activ-
ity (ACT), and sequence diagrams (SD)—capture system dynamics
such as interactions, control flows, and state transitions [ 54]. UCDs
specify "what" the system interacts with external actors rather than
"how." STM, ACT, and SD capture internal behavior and control
flow that underpin correctness and performance in safety-critical
domains. This study focuses on these three diagram types. Despite
SysML’s strengths [ 68,69], modelers need to build models by drag-
ging and dropping graphical elements, a slow process even for
simple cases, which raises the cost of MDD and discourages indus-
trial adoption.Improving modeling efficiency while maintaining
quality is crucial for broader adoption in safety-critical domains.
Recent advances in large language models(LLMs) are opening
new avenues for automating software engineering [ 78]. LLMs per-
form strongly in natural-language understanding [ 20] and code
generation [ 50], but their use for SysML model generation remains
nascent yet promising [ 3]. Translating textual requirements into
standardized behavioral models could substantially reduce man-
ual efforts [ 23]. Apvrille and Sultan [ 3] conducted a preliminary
evaluation in which ChatGPT-assisted Block Diagrams and STM
were slightly better than student-built models. Tinnes et al. [ 70]
explored the performance of LLMs in model generation and comple-
tion. Ferrari et al. [ 23] investigated LLM support for constructing
SD. Across these studies, completeness and correctness with re-
spect to the stated requirements remain recurring challenges. This

work investigates why LLMs succeed or fail at behavioral diagrams

proposes integrating modeling-language knowledge and model
checking to ensure high-quality automation.
We conducted an empirical study of LLMs performance and hal-
lucinations when generating three SysML behavioral diagrams type
across multiple domains.To enable this, we curated a dataset cover-
ing these diagrams. Because safety-critical sectors(e.g., aerospace)
restrict data sharing [ 31,48], we sourced models from open-source
projects, SysML standards [ 53,54], and authoritative books [ 28,62].
The artifacts were heterogeneous and often lacked requirements,
so we validated PlantUML compliance, SysML syntax and grammar,
and requirement consistency, reverse-engineering requirements
when absent. After 524 hours of collection and processing by mod-
eling professionals, we compiled a dataset of 107 validated models.
We evaluate LLMs on generating SysML behavioral diagrams
from natural-language requirements. Phase I designs a prompt
template that enforces SysML grammar/semantics, PlantUML for-
matting, and requirements alignment, with supplied exemplar mod-
els. Over 70% of models were generated within 10 s, achieving
>92% PlantUML format accuracy, >90% SysML grammar correct-
ness, and >90% requirement consistency for activity diagrams. We
analyzed hallucinations and devised model-checking rules. Phase-
IIintegrates these rules into a regeneration step, correcting most
formatting (35/37) and grammar (22/25) issues but only modestly
improved semantic hallucinations (31/72) and consistency (56/150).
Finally, we examine causes of hallucination, propose richer feed-
back (e.g., counterexamples, simulation traces), and high cost of
model checking as a ongoing challenge.
This study makes two key contributions to the research on
LLMs and model-driven engineering. (1) Publicly SysML behavior
model dataset. We release a curated dataset of 107 SysML behavior
models (36 activity, 36 state machine, and 35 sequence diagrams)
in PlantUML format. Drawn from peer-reviewed papers, textbooks,
and open-source projects across multiple domains, each model
is paired with its corresponding natural-language requirements.
The dataset supports LLMs-enabled MDD research and is available
here1. (2)Empirical evaluation of LLM performance and hal-
lucinations in SysML behavior model generation. Addressing
the scarcity of systematic evaluations [ 23], we assess generation
quality and categorize hallucination types. Model-checking rules
effectively remove syntactic defects (grammar and format), but se-
mantic issues persist, clarifying current limitations and pointing to
future research directions.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2
reviews related work. Section 3 describes the construction of our
model dataset. Section 4 outlines the research method. Section 5 and
Section 6 present and analyze the empirical study results. Section 7
discusses threats to validity. Section 8 concludes the paper.
2 Related Work
SysML-based system modeling remains costly and time-consuming.
Before LLMs, "intelligent" modeling largely relied on traditional
1https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/10eo8KDqlBlkQZxPpPCB7R3-
aBQZ7Rsm6?usp=drive_linknatural language processing and deep learning techniques. Gel-
hausen [ 27] and Bashir [ 9] proposed rule-based conversions from
natural-language requirements to UML Class Diagrams. Ibrahim [ 36]
and More [ 49] introduced domain ontologies to reduce ambiguity
and improve concept recognition. Deva [ 19,41] and Chemi [ 12]
combined object-oriented analysis with syntactic parsing to gen-
erate UML class diagrams or SysML Block Definition Diagrams.
These NLP-based methods depend on hand-crafted rules, input con-
straints, or bespoke architectures to cope with language informality,
and thus suffer from low recognition accuracy, heavy input formal-
ization, and limited domain knowledge - reducing generalizability
across modeling scenarios.
Recent adcanves in LLMs have spurred interest in software mod-
eling tasks. Table 1 surveys recent LLM-based modeling approaches.
In requirements modeling, Jin et al. [ 39] systematically evaluated
seven state-of-the-art LLMs on the CPSBENCH dataset to gener-
ate Cyber-Physical System (CPS) Problem Diagrams from natural
language requirements. Results show promise, but limited domain-
knowledge integration and frequent hallucinations hinder practical
adoption, constraining applicability in industrial settings that de-
mand high-precision specifications.
In design model generation, Conrardy et al. [ 15] showed GPT
reliably converts hand-drawn UML Class Diagrams to PlantUML
format, but syntactic and semantic errors grow with diagram com-
plexity. Cámara et al. [ 16] reported persistent syntactic defects and
semantic ambiguities in GPT-generated textual UML models, espe-
cially for large-scale diagrams (>12 classes) or complex concepts.
Ahmad et al. [ 1] evaluated GPT as an architecture assistant (DevBot)
in human-AI co-design and found strategy biases that conflicted
with specific requirements. Wang et al. [ 72] found LLMs help UML
novices identify basic elements but struggle with inter-class rela-
tionships and complex semantics, necessitating human supervision.
Bader et al. [ 6] automated XMI code generation for UML Compo-
nent Diagrams but noted reliability issues in medium-to-high com-
plexity tasks due to XMI verbosity and context window limitations.
Ferrari et al. [ 23] showed GPT can produce standard-compliant
UML Sequence Diagrams from natural language, improving con-
sistency and clarity yet falling short on behavioral completeness
and scenario coverage. Apvrille et al. [ 4] integrated iterative model-
checking feedback into the TTool-AI framework to enhance SysML
Block and Internal Block Diagram generation, though effectiveness
was constrained by LLM context processing limits. Tinnes et al.
[70] formalized software model evolution using change graphs, au-
tomating UML/SysML/Ecore model completion via GPT, historical
model data, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Experi-
mental results show that LLMs perform well in model evolution
tasks, though noise and lack of context often cause deviations from
expected results.
In conclusion, while existing research focuses on model genera-
tion and completion, no prior work has systematically investigated
LLMs’ effectiveness and hallucinations in SysML behavior model
generation. This study fills that gap with rigorous empirical method-
ologies.

3 Dataset Construction
3.1 Status of Missing SysML dataset
To identify relevant datasets, we searched English and Chinese
literature via Google Scholar [ 29] and CNKI [ 52], and explored
open-source projects on GitHub [ 46]. Only three studies mention
SysML datasets [ 11,66,81]. Spangelo et al. [ 66] used a SysML model
repository from a train product line in Magic Draw, containing
100 annotated models for training named entity recognition [ 11].
Unfortunately, the datasets from this work, as well as those from
the other two studies, have not been made publicly available.
3.2 Dataset Construction Group
We have two groups: G_Search for data collection (G_Search) and
G_Model for data processing & modeling. G_Search, with four se-
nior undergraduates and one master’s student, handled data acquisi-
tion. G_Model, comprising one senior undergraduate, two master’s
students, and two doctoral candidates, focused on data refinement
and model construction. All members had software engineering
backgrounds and MDD expertise, each with over 100 hours of mod-
eling experience in system/software projects, ensuring high-quality
data collection, processing, and model development.

4 Design of Empirical Study
4.1 Research Questions
RQ1: How effective are LLMs in generating SysML behavior
models, and what common hallucinations occur in their out-
puts? Our goal is to assess the effectiveness and hallucinations
of LLMs in generating SysML behavior models. We will evaluate
the quality of generated models, generation time, and overall per-
formance using different LLMs, while also analyzing the types of
hallucinations observed in the results.RQ2: How does model checking feedback help reduce hal-
lucinations in SysML behavior models generated by LLMs?
We aim to address hallucinations in behavior models generated
by LLMs. Based on the results from RQ1, we propose syntax and
semantic checking rules and integrate the feedback into the prompt
to guide model regeneration. Additionally, we will analyze how
this feedback impacts the quality of models generated by different
LLMs.
4.2 Evaluation Process
Figure 3 summarizes two-phase experimental design. Phase-I eval-
uates LLMs on SysML behavioral diagrams generation and iden-
tifies potential hallucinations. We employ a RAG component to
retrieve relevant PlantUML format and SysML v1.6 specification
text, and we structure requirements plus auxiliary context into a
prompt template. Outputs are assessed by time-to-generate, Plan-
tUML formatting accuracy, SysML grammar conformance, semantic
consistency (F1-score), and hallucination taxonomy. Hallucinations
are grouped into four types: PlantUML formatting errors, SysML
grammar errors, SysML semantic inconsistencies, and requirements
semantic inconsistencies.
Phase-II corrects hallucinations via regeneration guided by
model-checking feedback. Detected issues and their associated rules
are processed by the same validator as in Phase 1. The resulting
feedback is injected into a revised prompt and sent to the LLM
for regeneration. Regenerated models are evaluated on the same
four dimensions. To avoid contamination, each regeneration runs
in a fresh LLM session. Phase-II implements four classes of model-
checking rules(detail below).
(1) PlantUML format checking. Validates conformance to
PlantUML syntax/format rules. Results are reported automatically
by the PlantUML model checker.
(2) SysML grammar checking. Assesses adherence to SysML
grammar. Because PlantUML lacks a SysML grammar checker, we
manually compare each item against the standard and record the
errors.
(3) SysML semantic checking. Assessed compliance with se-
mantics defined in the SysML standard. As PlantUML does not
support semantic validation, we manually check the model against
55 semantics and log violations.2
(4) Requirements semantic checking. We define this as eval-
uating semantic consistency between the generated model and
a reference model. In code generation, syntactic/structural accu-
racy is usually measured via static analysis (e.g., AST matching [ 2],
CodeBLEU [ 60], ROUGE-L [ 42], METEOR [ 7], ChrF [ 57]), while
functional correctness uses metrics such as Pass@k [ 13]. For model
generation, grammar accuracy is split into PlantUML format accu-
racy (𝐴𝑐𝑐𝑃) and SysML grammar accuracy ( 𝐴𝑐𝑐𝑆), while functional
correctness typically requires simulation-based validation [ 25,45].
In this study, we assume the reference model is semantically correct
and compute the F1-score between the generated and reference
models to quantify requirement semantic alignment.

4.4 Modeling Tool Selection
Table 4 summarizes our survey of 10 SysML modeling tools, evaluat-
ing their support for SysML v1.6, data format openness, and accessi-
bility. Only Papyrus and PlantUML satisfied all criteria. We selected
PlantUML for our experiments due to its simpler and more intu-
itive format compared to Papyrus’s XMI representation.Although
the selected PlantUML tool is user-friendly and complies with the
SysML v1.6 specification, it lacks built-in capabilities for SysML
syntax and semantic checking. Consequently, all validations must
be performed manually, potentially limiting the comprehensiveness
and automation of the research. Future studies are recommended to
utilize more powerful SysML modeling tools or develop specialized
automated model-checking tools to enhance the comprehensive-
ness and accuracy of evaluations.

Requirements(Req) :Requirements play a key role in guiding
the LLM to complete various modeling tasks. They are divided into
three main parts: (1) Output Format : We built our dataset based
on the PlantUML format. We require that the generated model ad-

instructions in the sample. (2) Output Content : We direct the LLM

corresponding behavioral model.

support, including SysML syntax and semantics, ensuring the LLM
has complete information for model generation.
Error(E) :We feed model checking results as error messages to
the LLM, highlighting issues to improve model accuracy.
We used greedy sampling [ 65] with a temperature setting of 0.
Greedy sampling ensures that the LLM selects the most likely result
at each step, thus avoiding the randomness associated with other
sampling methods and guaranteeing the consistency and reliability
of generated responses.

The experimental results reveal substantial variation across model
types. ACT performs the best, approaching human-level quality,
while SD is the most challenging, with only about half of the inter-
actions correctly generated. SD modeling requires a deeper under-
standing of message-passing sequences, which cannot be achieved
through natural language comprehension alone—highlighting the
need for additional support tools or structured guidance.

6.4 Findings in RQ2
The results show that model-checking rules effectively correct syn-
tactic hallucinations, addressing structural and grammar errors and
ensuring LLMs follow explicit grammar rules. However, seman-
tic hallucinations in SysML have a low resolution rate of 36.89%,highlighting LLMs’ difficulty with SysML semantics and reasoning.
For requirement consistency, ACT achieves an 83.33% resolution
rate, STM 42.14%, and SD only 16.67%. Model-checking’s impact on
complex reasoning is limited. As rule complexity increases, LLMs
may lose focus on the original requirements, and fixing one issue
can introduce new ones.
One key reason is the lack of an optimal model-checking
feedback mechanism in our study. To overcome these limita-
tions, formal verification techniques present a promising solution.
By automatically verifying model correctness, they can detect er-
rors and generate counterexamples. These counterexamples can be
provided as feedback to the LLM, aiding in refining its output and
improving focus during regeneration. However, we have also noted
that the cost associated with model checking is relatively high. Due
to theoretical constraints inherent in model checking, verifying a
specific property or functionality may require substantial time, and
in some cases, may not yield a conclusive verification result.
7 Threat to Validity
Threats to Construct Validity stem from our goal of evaluat-
ing LLMs’ performance in generating SysML behavioral models.
To ensure an accurate assessment, the dataset must capture the
complexity of SysML behavioral modeling. We addressed this by
collecting a large, diverse dataset, including models from industrial
providers. Threats to Internal Validity stem from our research
design. The first threat is the reconstruction of behavioral model re-
quirements. For models without explicit requirements, we manually
created them, which is subjective. To reduce bias, we cross-checked
the reconstructed requirements for consistency with the models.
The second threat concerns LLM configuration. Model performance
can vary with different inference frameworks. To control this, we
used a greedy decoding strategy and set the temperature to 0, mini-
mizing randomness and ensuring consistent behavior. Threats to
External Validity relate to the generalizability of our findings. The
first threat concerns the representativeness of our dataset. To ad-
dress this, we gathered a diverse set of SysML behavior models from
various domains, including rail transit, aerospace, and equipment
manufacturing, ensuring broad real-world coverage. Although this
paper has constructed a SysML model dataset spanning multiple
domains, due to data sensitivity and confidentiality issues in safety-
critical domains, the number of models available in certain fields
is limited. Future research should further expand the sample size
within each domain or collaborate with industry to obtain a wider
range of real-world cases, thereby enhancing the generalizability
of the research conclusions. The second threat is our selection of
LLMs. We chose widely used models from leading companies and
open-source communities, all with accessible APIs for practical use
and integration in real-world applications.

Our work shows that when LLMs are given PlantUML format spec-
ifications and SysML syntax rules, their syntactic accuracy exceeds
90%. By providing requirement descriptions and additional data
format information, LLM-generated models in ACT tasks show
strong consistency with human-authored models. We summarize
